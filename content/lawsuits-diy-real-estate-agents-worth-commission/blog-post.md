# Lawsuits, DIY Real Estate, and Whether Your Agent Is Actually Worth the Commission

**TL;DR —** After the 2024 commission-related lawsuits, real estate commissions are openly negotiable on both sides of the deal. You *can* buy or sell a home on your own, but California is one of the most paperwork-heavy real estate markets in the country (42+ seller disclosures alone). Whether an agent is worth it comes down to one question: *can this person actually get the job done and protect you while they do it?* If you're a California seller, that's exactly what I do.

## Key Takeaways

- The 2024 commission lawsuits forced the industry to be transparent about what insiders already knew: **commissions are negotiable on both sides** of a transaction.
- "Flashy agent" energy — luxury cars, vacation reels, designer-everything — is not a signal that the agent can actually sell your house.
- You **can** sell or buy a home on your own. In California, you'll be navigating 42+ general disclosures on the seller side and another stack on the buyer side.
- The single most effective consumer-protection habit in any real estate transaction is to **follow up every verbal conversation with a short email** that documents what was said.
- Before closing, buyers should **door-knock the neighbors who share a property line** with the home they're buying. It's the cheapest, most informative inspection there is.

## What actually changed after the 2024 real estate commission lawsuits?

**Short answer (≤60 words):** The 2024 commission-related lawsuits and the NAR settlement era forced agents and brokerages to be openly transparent that commissions are negotiable — on the listing side *and* the buyer side. Buyers now sign separate buyer-representation agreements before touring homes, and listing agreements have to spell out commission terms clearly.

For most of my 28 years in real estate, the negotiability of commission was an open secret inside the industry. Some agents handled it the right way — they told sellers, "Here's the commission, here's what it pays for, and yes, it's negotiable." Other agents weren't as upfront. That's what the lawsuits centered on.

Like I said in the show — I spent 20 years with LAPD before I started selling real estate full time. One spoiled apple ruins the bunch. Real estate had its spoiled apples, the lawsuits called them out, and now we all operate under more transparent rules. That's a good thing.

## Can I sell or buy a house in California without a real estate agent?

**Short answer (≤60 words):** Yes. In California you can list your home as For Sale By Owner (FSBO) or represent yourself as a buyer. But California is one of the most document-heavy real estate markets in the country — there are 42+ general seller disclosures, plus buyer-side disclosures and warnings. Doing it yourself is legal; doing it well is the hard part.

Think about it the same way you'd think about representing yourself in court. It's allowed. The judge will probably look at you and say, "You'd really be better off hiring someone who's trained, experienced, and credentialed." Most people, faced with that, get the attorney. A small percentage don't — and some of them do just fine.

Real estate is the same. If you want to do it yourself, you can. I make videos on exactly how to do that — both for FSBO sellers and for self-represented buyers — because I think the consumer deserves to know the steps either way.

## How do I tell if a real estate agent is actually worth the commission?

**Short answer (≤60 words):** Look at what they post and what they do, not what they drive. An agent worth the commission consistently produces content and conversation that helps buyers and sellers make better decisions and protects them through the deal. An agent who mostly posts vacation photos, luxury cars, and lifestyle reels is selling themselves, not you.

Here's the test I'd use if I were hiring an agent today:

1. **Look at their last 30 days of public content.** Is it educational and protective — or is it a highlight reel of their lifestyle?
2. **Ask how commission works at their brokerage.** A good agent will tell you, plainly, that it's negotiable on both sides and explain what their fee actually pays for.
3. **Ask them to walk you through the disclosure packet.** In California that's a real test — there's a lot of paperwork, and a knowledgeable agent can explain it without flinching.
4. **Watch how they document conversations.** A pro will follow up verbal discussions with an email recap. If they don't, you should.

## What is the single best way to protect yourself in a real estate transaction?

**Short answer (≤60 words):** After any verbal conversation with your agent, lender, escrow officer, or inspector — send a short follow-up email that summarizes what was said and asks them to confirm. This creates a written record that protects you if something goes wrong months later. It costs nothing and works in every state.

This works even — *especially* — with people you trust. Maybe your lender is your uncle. Maybe your agent is a friend of the family. Doesn't matter. Send the email anyway. "Hey, just confirming our conversation today — you said X, Y, and Z. Let me know if I have any of that wrong." If they correct something, now you have the corrected version in writing too.

If you ever end up in front of a judge — over a disclosure dispute, a financing issue, a boundary problem — that email trail is the difference between "he said / she said" and a documented record.

## What's the one move almost no buyer makes before closing?

**Short answer (≤60 words):** Door-knock the neighbors who share a property line with the home you're buying — *before* you close escrow. Bring your agent. Meet the people on either side and behind. You'll learn about barking dogs, shared fences, parties, property-line issues, and neighborhood gossip you'll never find in a disclosure packet.

This is in no contract. It's on no disclosure. But it's the single highest-information, lowest-cost thing a buyer can do during escrow.

Here's what you're looking for:

- **Who lives there?** Owner-occupied, renters, recent move-in?
- **Is there a dog?** If a 90-pound German Shepherd is launching at the front door, that dog is going to be in the backyard — *your* property line — at some point.
- **Shared fences or trees.** Leaning fence? Big tree dropping roots? Now's the time to find out if the neighbor would split a future repair.
- **Vibe check.** Bikers, college kids, a quiet retiree, somebody's ex with a restraining order against you from 1985 — all useful information, in very different ways.

The neighbor who'll talk your ear off for 45 minutes? That's actually the *best* one to find. They know everything. Let them.

And if your agent won't walk those steps with you — that tells you something about your agent.

## What this means if you're selling a home in California right now

If you're a California seller in 2026, three things matter more than ever:

1. **Commission is negotiable** — and your listing agreement should spell it out clearly. Don't sign something you don't understand.
2. **Disclosures are the heart of a clean sale.** 42+ general disclosures is not a number to be afraid of — it's a number to be *prepared for*. The cleaner your disclosure package, the less risk of a post-close lawsuit.
3. **The agent you hire should be protective, not promotional.** You want someone whose job is to keep you out of trouble, not someone whose job is to keep their feed full.

That's the entire job. That's what I do.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Are real estate commissions still negotiable in California after the 2024 lawsuits?
Yes. Commissions have always been legally negotiable, and the 2024 commission lawsuits and the NAR settlement made it mandatory for agents and brokerages to communicate that openly. Both the listing-side and buyer-side commissions are negotiable.

### Do I have to sign a buyer-representation agreement before touring homes now?
In most cases, yes. After the 2024 settlement era, buyers typically sign a written buyer-representation agreement with their agent — including the agreed-upon commission — before being shown homes. The exact requirements vary by brokerage and state.

### How many disclosures does a seller have to provide in California?
California sellers complete roughly 42 general disclosures for a standard residential resale, plus property-specific addenda. Buyers receive additional informational and educational disclosures. It is one of the most document-heavy residential markets in the United States.

### Is For Sale By Owner (FSBO) legal in California?
Yes. California sellers may list and sell their property without a real estate agent. They are still responsible for completing all required disclosures and following state and federal real estate law. Many FSBO sellers hire a transaction coordinator or real estate attorney to assist with paperwork.

### What is a "sellers only" real estate agent?
A sellers-only agent is a licensed real estate agent who exclusively represents home sellers. They do not take buyer clients. The model avoids dual-agency conflicts and focuses all of the agent's marketing, pricing, and negotiation effort on the seller side of the transaction.

### Should buyers really door-knock the neighbors before closing?
Yes. Talking to the neighbors on either side and behind a home you're buying — before closing escrow — surfaces issues no disclosure packet will: barking dogs, fence and tree disputes, neighborhood vibe, and shared-boundary concerns. It is one of the highest-value, lowest-cost steps a buyer can take.

### How can I protect myself in real estate conversations with my agent or lender?
After every important verbal conversation, send a short follow-up email summarizing what was said and asking the other party to confirm. This creates a written record that protects you if a dispute arises later. Do this even with people you trust.

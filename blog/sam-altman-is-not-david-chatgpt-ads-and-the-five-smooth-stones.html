Sam Altman Is Not David: ChatGPT Ads, Five Smooth Stones, and the Verse the Press Skipped

What OpenAI Actually Launched

Walk through the tech press this week and we will read the same frame written six different ways. OpenAI opened up its advertising platform. Seven hundred million weekly users. Cost-per-click bidding. No minimum spend. A self-serve dashboard that mirrors the buying experience of Google Ads. And then, inevitably, the comparison: Sam Altman as the slim challenger with a sling, taking aim at the lumbering Google ad business that has owned search advertising for twenty-five years.

The little guy with a new weapon. The giant with a fortress of data. David and Goliath. The story has been told so many times in business writing that nobody pauses to even read it anymore.

We are going to pause and read it.

Because the people building these systems are using the language of the Bible without the substance of the Bible. They are taking the shape of a story and throwing away the source. And for a Christian small business owner watching all of this unfold, trying to figure out where to stand, the difference is the entire point.

The Verse the Press Skipped

First Samuel chapter seventeen, verse forty, in the King James Version:

And he took his staff in his hand, and chose him five smooth stones out of the brook, and put them in a shepherd's bag which he had, even in a scrip; and his sling was in his hand: and he drew near to the Philistine. 1 Samuel 17:40

Five stones. Read it again. That is five.

Most of our lives we have heard the David and Goliath story told as the story of one stone. One shot. One throw. One faith-fueled crack against the brow of a giant. And then a person reads the text instead of the campfire version and notices the count. Five. He took five. Why did he take five?

The verse does not tell us in that line. But the Bible does tell us, several books later. Second Samuel chapter twenty-one, verses fifteen through twenty-two, and again in First Chronicles chapter twenty, verse five: Goliath had relatives. Brothers. Sons of the giant. Men of unusual stature. One of them, a man named Lahmi, gets killed by Elhanan in a later battle. Another, including a man with six fingers on each hand and six toes on each foot, falls by the hand of David and his servants.

David did not bring five stones because he doubted God could land the first one. He brought five because he had spent his boyhood as a shepherd. And a shepherd who lives by the sling knows two things. He knows that a round stone flies true and a chipped one veers off. And he knows that the giant in front of him has brothers.

The five stones were not a hedge against the failure of God. They were the natural arsenal of a faithful shepherd who had thought through what came after. In the original story, faith and preparation are the same act. They are not opposites. The shepherd boy who trusts the Lord is the same shepherd boy who picks up the smoothest five stones in the brook.

The Line Before the Throw

Hold that picture in mind and consider the man the press wants to cast as David in May of 2026.

Sam Altman is the chief executive of OpenAI. He has access to hundreds of billions of dollars in venture commitments. He has compute deals with Microsoft and Oracle, with the largest data center operators on earth. He has spoken publicly and repeatedly about building artificial general intelligence. He has projected one hundred billion dollars per year in advertising revenue by 2030. He is, by every measure available, a man of enormous accumulated power.

He is not a shepherd boy. And the question on the table is not whether his ad platform will work. The answer is that it probably will work, at least well enough to disrupt the ad economy, and the small business operator who gets in early is going to see real results. The question on the table is whether the framing the industry is using to describe what he is doing is accurate to the story the industry keeps borrowing.

And the answer is no.

Read on, because the verse the press never quotes is the one that makes the whole story make sense. First Samuel seventeen, verse forty-five:

Thou comest to me with a sword, and with a spear, and with a shield: but I come to thee in the name of the LORD of hosts, the God of the armies of Israel, whom thou hast defied. 1 Samuel 17:45

That is the part of the frame the framing strips out. That is the part that does not appear in a TechCrunch headline. That is the part that does not fit on a Sequoia Capital deck.

David did not win because he was scrappy. David won because the Lord of hosts had decided the day. The sling was the means. The Lord was the cause. Remove that and we no longer have a David story. We have a marketing case study about disruption theory dressed up in a costume.

And that is precisely what the press is handing us right now.

The Costumed David

The man casting himself as David in this story has publicly said, in interviews and in writings, that the goal of his work is to build a system that approaches or surpasses human intelligence in every domain. He has, in moments of unusual candor, used language about creation, about emergence, about the development of a kind of mind that does not exist in the world.

He is not building a sling. He is building, or trying to build, a god in the machine. That is not the work of a shepherd. That is the work of the men of Shinar in Genesis chapter eleven.

And they said, Go to, let us build us a city and a tower, whose top may reach unto heaven; and let us make us a name, lest we be scattered abroad upon the face of the whole earth. And the LORD came down to see the city and the tower, which the children of men builded. Genesis 11:4-5

The Lord came down to see that tower. He did not commend it.

Every business writer who pulls the David and Goliath frame out of the toolbox to describe a startup versus an incumbent is doing the same trick. They are taking the shape of the story and throwing away the source. The shape is the small thing beating the big thing. That part survives. That part is what makes the frame so durable in marketing copy.

The source is the line David spoke before the stone left the sling. The source is the Lord of hosts. The source is the part the press cannot copy into a launch announcement, because if it copied the source, the press would have to admit that the entire frame collapses without it.

This is not a small thing. This is the central thing. A real David carries the source of the sling, not just the sling. And the man wielding the new ad platform inside ChatGPT, however brilliant, however successful, however well-meaning some of his work may be, has not claimed that source. The press has thrust on him a moral arc he has not asked for.

What to Do With This

So what does a small business owner, a real estate agent, a contractor, a tradesman, a parent, do with this? Standing in May of 2026, watching ChatGPT roll out an ad platform, watching Google rush a response, watching the next platform get built by a company that does not exist as we type this sentence. Where do we stand?

We use the platform. That is not the question. Tools are tools.

The same Lord who put copper in the ground for plumbers and silicon in the ground for chip makers is sovereign over the use of a person who makes either. The carpenter of Nazareth used a hammer, and the hammer did not need the moral framework. The carpenter did.

What we do not do is mistake the man wielding the platform for David of scripture. We do not assign him a moral arc he has not claimed. We do not pray to a system because the system speaks fluently. We do not look at a man who is publicly working to build a synthetic mind and call him a shepherd boy fighting for the armies of the living God. He is not. He has not been asked to be. The press has thrust this role on him because the press needs a hero shape, and the David and Goliath frame is sitting in the drawer, free for the taking.

The shape is in the drawer. The source is not. And the watching person is the one who has to keep the two separated.

Your Own Five Stones

If we are Christians watching the rollout of ChatGPT advertising in 2026, there is a way to walk through it with hands clean and heads straight. Pick up our own five stones. They are different stones than the press will hand us. But they are the ones a real David carries.

  1. Faith

    The Lord owns the cattle on a thousand hills. The user of data, of seven hundred million weekly chat sessions, of trillion-parameter language models, does not change that. The tools change. The owner of the field does not. Build the campaign. Trust the field's owner.

  2. Preparation

    David picked up the smoothest stones in the brook. Measured. Because a shepherd knows his weapon. We learn the platform. We read the documentation. We understand what we are using, whether that is Claude, ChatGPT, OpenAI's API, Meta's stack through Facebook and Instagram, Grok, or whatever the next company to ship ships. Faith without preparation is presumption. Preparation without faith is just hustle. We need both stones in the bag.

  3. Discernment

    Not everything new is good. Not everything new is bad. The question is not whether to use the sling. The question is what name we call on when we draw it. We do not become the operator who uses the language of David while serving the gods of Gath. The Bible has a name for that. It is not flattering.

  4. Restraint

    The same platform that brings us customers can be used to manipulate them. To mislead them. To build offers that prey on intent rather than serve it. The early advertisers will set the cultural norms of this channel for the next decade. We choose to be the kind of advertiser the Father would not be ashamed of. Restraint is a stone. Carry it.

  5. The Name

    David said it out loud. I come to thee in the name of the Lord of hosts. That sentence is the only part of the David and Goliath story the tech press cannot copy into a launch announcement. That is the part we keep. It is the part that makes the small operator using new tools an actual descendant of the shepherd boy and not a costumed reenactor.

Pick up those five. Walk into the brook. Read the platform documentation. Build the landing page. Send the ad. And do it in a name that is not Sam Altman's.

What "No Minimum Spend" Actually Means

Pull the strategy view down to ground level for a minute. What does it actually mean that ChatGPT opened a self-serve ad platform with no minimum spend? It means the real estate agent in Santa Clarita with two listings can run a campaign next to a Fortune 500 brand. It means the plumber, the contractor, the bookkeeper, the food-addiction-recovery coach, the dispensational pastor with a Substack, all of them can put an offer in front of a slice of seven hundred million weekly conversations without writing a check to a media buyer first.

That is real. That is not nothing. The small operator who learns this platform in the first ninety days of its existence will compound the advantage for years. The early advertisers always do. The cost-per-click stays lower while the inventory is uncrowded. The data on what works comes back faster because the auction is thin. The ad creative norms are not yet set, which means the operator who decides to write honest copy can stand out in a way that will become impossible once the channel matures.

All of that is genuinely good for the small Christian operator. That is the value to extract. That is the stone in the brook.

What we do not do is confuse the platform with a savior. The platform will not give us our purpose. The platform will not write our doctrine. The platform will not tell us who to be. The platform is copper. The platform is silicon. The platform is a thing in the ground that the Lord put there for us to shape into something useful. And the shaping is on us.

The Counterfeit Pattern

Watch what happens in the comment sections this week. Watch the LinkedIn posts. Watch the podcast clips. A wave of Christian-flavored marketing is about to roll over this story. People in pastels and crosses and verse overlays are going to record videos calling Sam Altman a modern David and the ChatGPT ad platform a gift from God to the persecuted small business. That is the counterfeit. That is the costumed David.

The counterfeit takes a real biblical story, strips the Lord of hosts out of it, and uses what is left as a marketing vehicle. The shape stays. The source goes. And once enough operators do this, the words themselves start to mean nothing. David becomes a synonym for scrappy founder. Goliath becomes a synonym for incumbent. Faith becomes a synonym for belief in your own roadmap. The text gets hollowed out from the inside and the average person reading a Christian small business influencer cannot tell anymore whether they are reading the Bible or a Sequoia pitch deck.

This is not new. The book of Judges warns about it. Paul warns about it. Jude warns about it. The pattern of stealing the shape of the gospel for a different use is older than the printing press. It is just easier now, because the printing press is in everybody's pocket and runs on ChatGPT credits.

The corrective is the same as it has always been. Read the text. Quote the source. Refuse to use David's name for anyone who does not call on David's God.

Faith and Preparation Are the Same Act

One of the worst readings of the David and Goliath story is the version that splits faith from preparation. The version that says David's faith was so total he did not need the stones, and the stones were a sign of his small remaining doubt. That reading does not survive the text.

David picked the stones first and spoke the line second. The verse order matters. Verse forty: he chose the five smooth stones. Verse forty-five: he names the Lord of hosts. Preparation came first in the sequence, faith ratified it before the throw. The two are one act in the heart of the shepherd.

This is the version a Christian operator needs to internalize in the age of AI. The faithful Christian small business owner does not throw open the laptop, type a prompt, and pray for the algorithm to bless it. The faithful Christian small business owner reads the documentation, learns what cost-per-click bidding actually does, studies who their customer is, builds the landing page so that it serves the customer rather than tricks them, and then runs the campaign under the watchful eye of the same God who watched David count stones at the bank of the brook.

Preparation is not a sign of weak faith. Preparation is what faith looks like when it has work to do.

Why the Frame Will Keep Coming

These platforms are going to keep coming. The next one will be Google's response to this one. The one after that will be something nobody has named yet, built by a company that does not exist as we type this sentence. And in every cycle, the press will reach for the David and Goliath frame because the press has a small toolkit and that is the most recognizable tool in it.

Read the Bible instead.

The shepherd boy in the valley did not win because he was clever. He won because the Lord of hosts decided to win through him. And the same Lord who decided that day is the Lord who decides this day. The names of the giants change. The Lord does not. He never does.

And the watching person stays watching. Sling in hand. Five stones in the bag. The Name on the tongue. Because God is not the machine. The machine was never going to be God. And the man who tries to build one will, in the end, be remembered for the same reason the builders at Babel are remembered. The Lord came down to see that tower too.

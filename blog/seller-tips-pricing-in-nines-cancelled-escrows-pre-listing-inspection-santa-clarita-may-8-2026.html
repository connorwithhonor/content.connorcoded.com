Spring and Summer in Santa Clarita: Why the Market Is Moving Now

Are you contemplating selling a house? I have a few tips for you sellers, and I will tell you up front who I am: I am a sellers-only agent. You can read more about that at sellersonlyagent.com. I have been listing exclusively, no buyer side, for the last seven years — and I sold real estate as a dual-direction agent for fourteen years before that. So this is not theory. This is what I have watched move 1,000-plus escrows in this valley.

As far as Santa Clarita goes, the market is starting to move a little bit every day. Anytime we get into the spring and summer time, in a market that is somewhat normal, you will see one of two things happen: the market really starts going up, or it really starts coming down. Right now, neither of those is true. Everything is a little static. We are watching the number of properties listed and the number of properties purchased both rise gently. That is what spring and summer do, and the reason is school.

School is a massive impact on family decisions, depending on where you happen to live. Santa Clarita Valley is a magnet for people looking to remove their kids from private school in the San Fernando Valley or elsewhere and put them into public school here. The public schools in Santa Clarita actually carry higher ratings than a lot of the private schools families are paying tuition to attend in the SFV. Walk through most neighborhoods in Stevenson Ranch, Saugus, Valencia, Castaic, Newhall, or Canyon Country and you will find elementary schools and even high schools with Blue Ribbon ratings. That means a lot to parents. It is the single biggest reason the spring-summer SCV market is what it is.

Then layer in price per square foot. Price per square foot for housing decreases as you get further away from city center. The full migration math — Sherman Oaks at roughly $697 per square foot vs Santa Clarita at $425, plus the $90,000 of after-tax private school tuition that disappears the day you move — is broken down in my May 2 SCV Market Watch. Santa Clarita Valley is not far. Our southern border is the northern border of the San Fernando Valley. We are minutes away. Different routes get you here depending on which freeway side you are on — the 5 or the 14. From the 14 side you have Sierra Highway as an alternative access. From the 5 side you have the freeway itself, plus Balboa, plus the Old Road / Foothill Boulevard, which splits and feeds into both directions. There are real ways out and back, and during the earthquake closure a few years ago we all saw why those alternates matter. Thank God for them.

The 90 Price Reductions in 7 Days — What That Number Is Really Telling You

This last week we have had approximately 90 price reductions on properties currently for sale in Santa Clarita. There are a few reasons that number matters, and the first one is the same reason I tell every seller the same uncomfortable thing.

If your property has been on the market and not moved in at least 30 days — you have been static at your particular price, you have had a few showings, no offers — it is time to re-evaluate. That does not necessarily mean you priced over the comps at the start. You might have matched up perfectly with the comparable sales the day you listed. But the market has taken a small hit since then. Interest rates moved up a quarter point. The buyer pool flexed. Two or three nearby comparable closings came in lower than what was active three weeks ago. The price you set on day one is no longer the price the market is reading on day 32.

Every one of those 90 price reductions is a seller doing the math: showings without offers, more days on market, comps shifting underneath, and a decision to meet the market where it actually is rather than where it was when the listing went live.

A price reduction is the market correcting your list price. It is not a defeat. It is a re-engagement.

How Much Should You Reduce? And Why You Have to Obey the Nines and Eights

How much you should reduce depends entirely on where you are priced right now. And there is a pricing convention that has been hiding in plain sight in retail and real estate forever, and you have to obey it. The nines and eights.

If you are listed at $760,000, you should never have been there. The right number was $759,900. I know that is only $100 different. I know it sounds silly. But every advertiser, every retailer, every car dealer, every grocery chain, and every successful real estate listing agent in this country uses nines and eights for a reason. Eights signal abundance and infinity. Nines signal value, sale, discount. They look like a deal subconsciously. They make a buyer feel they are getting under the next round number. Is it a game? Not really. It is a pricing strategy that has been studied to death and proven over and over.

Now here is the practical application when you are reducing. The single most powerful price reduction you can make is the one that crosses a psychological round number. If you are listed at $760,000, the meaningful reduction is to $749,900 — not $755,000. The first cracks the seven-five barrier. The second still reads as “in the seven sixties” to a buyer scanning a search results page on Zillow.

The same logic scales up. If you are at $810,000 and the comps and the lack of activity are telling you to come down, do not come to $800,000. Come to $799,900. The buyer searching with a $800K cap was filtering you out completely; now you are in their results.

Three factors should drive how big the reduction is, and they should be on the table with your agent before you make it:

  • Comparable sales. The closed comps in the last 60 to 90 days, weighted heavily toward the most recent. Active competition is information, but closed sales are evidence.
  • Non-movement. How many days you have been static, how many showings you have had, and how many of those generated written feedback that was not an offer.
  • Competition. What is currently active in your tract or your zip at your square footage and beds/baths, and where you sit in their stack on price.

I would rather see the reduction be $10,000 well-placed than $20,000 poorly placed. $760K to $749,900 is a $10,100 reduction that crosses a barrier. $760K to $740K is a $20,000 reduction that does not.

About a Third of Escrows Cancel. Here Is Why That Is Almost Always a Buyer Issue.

Here is a number that surprises most sellers when I tell them: about one third of real estate listings that go into escrow fall out of escrow. That is national data, and it is roughly what we see in Santa Clarita too in any given month.

Why? Most of the reasons are buyer issues. They really do not have anything to do with the seller. Now, sometimes a buyer comes back with a request for repairs that is unreasonable, and that is a discussion you and your agent have to make a call on. But the majority of cancellations have nothing to do with the home and everything to do with what the buyer did to themselves between contract acceptance and the close of escrow. I am going to walk you through the most common ones, because if you understand them as a seller you will know how to read the warning signs and you will know what to do when they appear.

Why I Will Not Take Both Sides of Your Deal (the Dual Agency Trap)

Before I get to the buyer-side mistakes, I have to say something about why I do not represent buyers at all anymore. When I did represent buyers — for fourteen years — I refused to do dual agency. Dual agency is the situation where one agent represents the seller and the buyer on the same property. The agent works for both sides.

It is a bipolar, weird, dreamy, nightmarish situation. By the time the buyer comes to me, I already have an intimate working relationship with the seller. I have told the seller I am going to fight for them. Then suddenly the same buyer who wants to use me to write the offer is the buyer I am supposed to fight against on negotiations, request for repairs, credits, and the closing. I cannot do both jobs at full strength simultaneously. Nobody can. The result is a watered-down representation for both sides, and it is bad for the seller, bad for the buyer, and lucrative only for the agent who collects both ends of the commission.

Seven years ago I went sellers-only and never looked back. Here is what that means for you as a seller: when an unrepresented buyer or a buyer with their own agent writes on your home, I have no conflict. I will negotiate with all the venom and vigor I have, because my loyalty is one direction and only one direction. The buyer has their own representation working for them. We meet in the middle and the deal closes harder and cleaner because of it.

The Ford Raptor Mistake (and the JCPenney Version of It)

Now to the buyer-side cancellation reasons. The single most common cause of an escrow blowing up at the lender level is a buyer doing something to their credit between offer acceptance and the closing wire.

Maybe they went out and bought the loaded Escalade or the fully loaded Ford Raptor. Maybe they walked into Pottery Barn or Williams-Sonoma or JCPenney and put a $10,000 bedroom set on a new store credit card — because they are coming from an apartment or out of a basement and they want to fill the new house. Maybe they applied for a new credit card or co-signed a vehicle loan for a family member. Any of those moves screws up the credit something awful, and there is almost nothing my best credit-repair contacts can do about it inside a 30-day escrow window.

Why? Because vehicle loans, big credit-card purchases, and new account applications all hit the FICO score and the debt-to-income ratio. The lender re-pulls credit a few days before close. If the buyer's score has dropped, or their debt-to-income has shifted, the loan that was approved on day one is no longer the loan that funds on day 30.

Here is the part that catches buyers and stuns sellers: even if the lender said it was okay, even if the lender said “go ahead and pay off that JCPenney card”, even if it is in writing — if it sinks the FICO, the deal is in trouble. The buyer goes back to the lender and says, “Jack, you said it was going to be okay.” Jack says, “What are you going to do?” They try to fix it. Most of the time they cannot. If the lender is a true direct lender they might squeeze something through. Mostly it is dead. The repercussions are limited — the buyer can sue, anyone can sue anyone for anything, but how far it goes and how much it costs is a different conversation. They can file a small claims action for damages. None of that gets the seller to close.

If you are a buyer reading this on the seller's blog: the rule is simple. While you are in escrow, do not buy anything, do not pay anything off, and do not apply for any new credit. If you absolutely have to, get the lender's blessing in writing. Not telephonically. Not by text. By email. Send the email yourself: “You said I could pay off the JCPenney card. You said I could buy this thing. You said I could apply for this credit. I want to confirm I heard that correctly. Please reply yes or no.” Wait for the reply. Save the reply. Then act.

How a Smart Seller Survives the One-in-Three Cancellation Rate

So a third of these deals fall apart. As a seller you should plan for it. Here is what that planning looks like.

Keep the Original Offer Pool Warm

If you had a competitive listing, you probably had multiple offers. Your agent talked the unsuccessful buyers and their agents off the ledge politely — "the seller chose another offer, thank you for writing." Most agents file those rejected offers in the trash and forget them. I do not.

I keep a running list of every agent and buyer who wrote on the property, with notes on price, terms, and any conditions they hinted they could flex on. If something starts to feel funny inside the active escrow — and you almost always know, because the buyer's agent goes dark — I am already mentally re-running that list.

The Going-Dark Warning Sign

Here is the warning sign that tells you the deal might be in trouble: the buyer's agent disappears. They do not return phone calls. They do not respond to texts. Time-sensitive items in the contract start to slip past their deadlines. It is not always disastrous — maybe they are sick, maybe they are at a hospital, maybe they had a family emergency — but enough times the going-dark behavior is the precursor to a cancellation that I treat it as a real signal.

What do you do when it happens? You call a third party. The lender that is doing the buyer's loan usually knows the agent — they have been talking through the financing — so you call the lender. Half the time the lender says, “Yeah, I have not heard from them either.” That tells you everything.

Then you tell the seller. You give them the rundown. Not panicked. Just the facts: "I went to the buyer's agent for X document. They are not communicating. The lender has not heard from them either. Here are the possibilities and here is what I am going to do next."

Notice to Perform — the Enforcement Mechanism

If the buyer side keeps dragging, your agent has a tool: the Notice to Perform. Once delivered, the buyer has a contractually defined window — typically 72 hours, three days — to act on whatever item is delinquent (deposit, contingency removal, signed disclosures, etc.). If they do not act, the seller has the right to cancel and keep the deposit on terms, depending on the specifics of the contract.

That is your insurance policy. Use it. The Notice to Perform is not a hostile move. It is the mechanism the contract specifically provides for exactly this scenario. A serious buyer responds. A buyer who has gone dark for a non-medical reason rarely does, and you protect yourself by not letting the deal drag past the timelines that protect you.

Re-List or Re-Engage?

When a deal cancels, your seller has to make one of two decisions:

  • Go back to the original offer pool. If you had multiple offers and you kept them warm, your agent makes those calls today. Sometimes you will get a fresh offer at a number close to the original price before the property even goes “active” again on the multiple listing service.
  • Go back on the open market. Re-list and resolicit the wider buyer pool through fresh marketing.

Some people are under the impression that a cancellation leaves a bad taste — that future buyers see a previously-pending property and pull back, wondering, “What did they discover? What skeletons are in the closet?” That is a real concern, and it is precisely why your agent has to do their due diligence and document why the cancellation happened. If it was a buyer-side credit problem, you can disclose that the cancellation had nothing to do with the home. If it was a property issue, then yes, you have to disclose it — and you have to address it.

The Pre-Listing Inspection — the Single Best Move a Seller Can Make

This is the move that separates the listings that close in 30 days from the listings that cancel out and have to come back to market with a stigma. The pre-listing inspection.

It costs you somewhere between $400 and $700 as a seller. It is worth its weight in gold. Here is what you do:

  1. Hire a certified, bonded, insured home inspector with strong references. Not the cheapest one. The one with credentials and experience. The one who will actually crawl the foundation, climb into the attic, and walk every accessible inch.
  2. Get the report. Read it cover to cover with your agent. There will be items you knew about. There will be items you had no idea existed. That is the entire point.
  3. Address what should be addressed using licensed, bonded, insured contractors with knowledge and experience. Health and safety items first. Then anything that is going to surface in a buyer's home inspection and become a credit fight at the request for repairs.
  4. Save every receipt and invoice. Every contractor invoice, every parts receipt, every permit. You are building a binder.
  5. Hand the binder to every offering buyer. The pre-listing inspection report. The list of items the seller addressed. The licensed contractors who did the work. The receipts. The remaining items the buyer is being given as a known condition.

Now think about what happens when a buyer writes on a home that comes pre-packaged like that. Their inspection becomes a confirmation, not a discovery. There are no surprises. The request for repairs is small or non-existent. The lender is happy because the appraisal comes in cleaner with documented updates. The deal closes on time.

This is the winning card for a real estate seller. If you are listing with me, you are getting this conversation on day one. If you are listing with someone else, ask them about it. If they shrug, that tells you something.

The Industry Is Consolidating — What That Means for You as a Seller

You have been seeing a lot of news lately about different real estate companies buying other companies, assuming brands, swallowing brokerages whole. It happens. Here is the part the headlines do not tell you: in a lot of those acquisitions, the agents get tossed to the side. Their book of business gets absorbed. Their listings get reassigned. The relationship the seller thought they had goes away on a Monday morning when the new ownership memo drops.

It is a difficult business. If you are working with somebody and they make it doubly stressful on you — if communication is poor, if your calls are not getting returned, if you are getting pillow talk where you do not really understand the gravity of what is happening in the transaction — rethink that alignment.

An agent should do their best to support you. They should also tell you the truth. As far as I am concerned, I would rather give it to you from the hip than smooth it over and have you discover the reality on day 27 of escrow. Communication between you and your agent is incredibly valuable. Make sure you can verify three things:

  • They communicate clearly. No jargon you do not understand. No vague answers. If you ask a question, you get an answer.
  • They communicate in the method you want. Some people want phone calls. Some want texts. Some want email. The agent works around your preference, not the other way around.
  • They communicate the truth, and they back it up in writing. Verbal promises are worth what they cost: nothing. Get it in writing. Pricing strategy in writing. Marketing plan in writing. Negotiation positions in writing. Email exists for a reason.

Putting It All Together: Your Sellers' Tip Recap

If you are a Santa Clarita seller in this market, here is the playbook in eight lines:

  1. Spring and summer is moving. The school cycle is the engine. Use the season; do not fight it.
  2. Price in nines and eights from day one. $759,900, not $760,000. $799,900, not $810,000.
  3. If you are static at 30 days, reduce by an amount that crosses a psychological barrier, validated by comps, non-movement, and the active competition.
  4. Plan for the one-in-three cancellation rate. Keep the original offer pool warm. Watch for the buyer's agent going dark.
  5. Use the Notice to Perform when the contract requires it. That is what it is there for.
  6. Tell your buyer (or have your agent tell their agent) that any new credit, vehicle loan, store card, or paid-off account during escrow is a deal-killer unless the lender approves it in writing by email.
  7. Order a pre-listing inspection. $400 to $700. Address what should be addressed with licensed contractors. Save the binder.
  8. Pick an agent who communicates clearly, in your preferred method, with the truth, in writing.

Do those eight things and you do not have to be the seller staring at a 33-day-old listing wondering why the showings stopped. You become the seller whose listing closes the way listings used to close: priced right, prepared right, handled right.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why are there 90 price reductions in Santa Clarita this week?

Most of those reductions trace to listings that priced at the comps on day one but did not adjust as comparable sales shifted lower or as interest rates ticked up over the following 30 days. A reduction is not a failure. It is a re-engagement with the market where it is now, not where it was when you listed.

Why do real estate prices use nines and eights?

Nines and eights are a pricing strategy retailers and real estate professionals use because round numbers feel like full price and numbers ending in nine feel like a value or a deal. Just as importantly, $759,900 shows up in a buyer's $750K-and-under search filter; $760,000 does not. That single difference can be the gap between a showing and no showing.

How much should I reduce my list price after 30 days static on the market?

The answer depends on three factors: the most recent closed comparable sales, the volume and quality of activity (showings, written feedback, offers) you have had, and where you sit relative to active competition. The most powerful reduction is one that crosses a psychological round number. $760K to $749,900 will outperform $760K to $755K every time, even though one is a smaller dollar move.

Why do a third of escrows cancel before close?

Most cancellations are buyer-side: changes to the buyer's credit during escrow (new vehicle loans, store cards, paid-off accounts that re-score the FICO), appraisal coming in below contract, or repair disputes the buyer will not resolve. Sellers can mitigate by ordering a pre-listing inspection, pricing accurately, and keeping the original offer pool warm in case the active deal fails.

What is a pre-listing inspection and is it worth it?

A pre-listing inspection is a $400 to $700 inspection the seller commissions before going live on the market. The inspector reviews the entire home and produces a report. The seller then addresses the items that should be addressed using licensed contractors and saves all receipts. The result is a transparent, well-documented listing that closes faster and avoids the request-for-repairs fights that kill so many deals. Yes, it is worth it. It is the single most underused tool in a seller's toolbox.

Why do you not represent buyers anymore?

I represented buyers for 14 years. The last seven I have been sellers-only. The reason is dual agency: when one agent represents both the seller and the buyer on the same property, the representation gets watered down on both sides. Going sellers-only lets me fight for one side with no conflict. The buyer has their own agent. We negotiate hard. The deal closes cleaner.

What is a Notice to Perform and when should it be used?

A Notice to Perform is a contractually-defined notice the seller can deliver when the buyer has missed a contractual deadline (deposit, contingency removal, signed disclosures, etc.). It opens a defined window — typically 72 hours — in which the buyer must act. If they do not, the seller has the right to cancel under the contract terms. It is not hostile; it is the enforcement mechanism the contract specifically provides.

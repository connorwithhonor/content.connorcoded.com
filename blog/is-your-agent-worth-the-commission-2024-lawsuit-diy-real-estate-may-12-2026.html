The Honest Answer in 90 Seconds

A lot changed in real estate after the 2024 commission lawsuit. The settlement did not invent the idea that commissions are negotiable — that has always been true — but it forced the industry to say so out loud, in writing, on both sides of every transaction. Below: what the lawsuit actually said, the difference between agents who are selling status and agents who are selling competence, when DIY real estate works and when California's 42-plus seller disclosures turn it into a part-time legal job, the email rule that protects you in every conversation with your agent or your lender, and the single buyer-side investigation almost no one does before close — the neighbor door-knock.

1998, Twenty-Eight Years, and the Lawsuits That Change an Industry

A lot has changed since I started selling real estate in 1998. There have been a few lawsuits, of course, and a few other things that have impacted real estate agents' basic flow. The lawsuits come about because people in the world of buying and selling homes feel slighted in some way. Larger law firms find those people, join them into a class action, and there is power in numbers. They take it to court. A jury of twelve, or a judge in a bench trial, decides whether the entity being sued is liable. If the decision goes against the industry, things start to change as a result.

That is exactly what happened in 2024. We watched as a massive lawsuit moved through the federal court system, alleging that commissions were not as negotiable in practice as the industry had been claiming they were. What I glean from the lawsuit (and you should read it yourself, not just my take) is that in some cases, agents were not being completely upfront and honest with their clients about commission structure.

I was in law enforcement for two decades before this. We had a saying: one spoiled apple can ruin the bunch. The same thing happened in real estate. We had some agents who were not doing it the right way — the professional way, the way that opens the conversation with the client and says, in writing, "Here is what the commission is, here is what it covers, and here is what it is negotiable on." Enough of them did not do that, and the courts noticed.

What the 2024 Commission Lawsuit Actually Did

I am going to keep this short and accurate because it is the kind of topic where people get sloppy and start making things up. Here are the practical changes that flow from the 2024 settlement, the ones you as a buyer or seller actually feel:

  • Commission is explicitly negotiable on both sides. Listing-side commission is negotiable between the seller and the listing agent. Buyer-side compensation is negotiable between the buyer and the buyer's agent. Neither is preset by the brokerage industry.
  • Buyer-side compensation is no longer automatically advertised on the MLS as a one-size-for-everyone number that the listing side promises to pay. It is a separate conversation now, in writing, between the buyer and their representation.
  • Written buyer representation agreements are standard before showings in most jurisdictions. A buyer signs an agreement with their agent that explains what the agent is being paid, by whom, and under what conditions, before they walk through a single property.
  • Sellers have direct visibility into and control over what they are offering, if anything, to a buyer's agent at the listing stage. The cooperating commission is now a separate strategic decision a seller makes with their listing agent, not a default assumption.

The reason this matters to you is that the lawsuit forced into the open a conversation that should have been in the open the whole time. If you are working with an agent today and they cannot walk you through their compensation in plain English, in writing, in the first meeting — that is information. It tells you what the rest of the transaction is going to feel like.

Commissions have always been negotiable. The 2024 lawsuit just forced the industry to say so out loud, in writing, on both sides. The agents who were already operating that way did not change a thing.

The Flash Agent vs the Substance Agent

If you have been a seller or buyer in the last twenty years, you have seen the pattern. You scroll Instagram, you scroll Facebook, you scroll LinkedIn, and the same image keeps showing up. The flashy clothes. The nice car. The nice purse. The nice wallet. The fancy watch. The vacations. The new listing in the foothills with the agent posing in front of it like it is their house. The "look how successful I am" content cycle that has dominated agent marketing for a generation.

Most consumers, when they really think about it, do not actually want that. That is not what draws a person to an agent. What draws a person to an agent is the agent who can do the job. When I am hiring a professional — an attorney, an accountant, a contractor, an inspector — I am not all that interested in how successful they look on social media. In some cases I might be a little suspicious of it. If they are doing that well, am I about to be the one paying for the next vacation?

There is a balance here. Some level of credibility signaling matters; you do not want an agent with no business and no track record. But the credibility you want is in the work product, not the wardrobe. Here is the test I would run on any agent before signing with them:

What is the agent posting?

  • Are they posting the Provost motor home and the latest NASCAR trip? That is status. Status is fine for a friend; status is not what protects you in a transaction.
  • Are they posting market data, breakdowns of disclosures, explanations of what active under contract versus pending means, walkthroughs of what a request for repairs looks like, the things a buyer or seller actually needs to know to avoid a mistake? That is competence. Competence is what protects you.

How does the agent communicate?

Send them an email with three specific questions about a transaction. See whether you get a clear, complete written answer or a "let's hop on a call" deflection. The agents who refuse to put things in writing are usually the agents whose mouths and contracts disagree later.

Can the agent put their fee structure in writing?

Ask, in writing, for the agent's fee for the engagement and the alternative structures they offer. A flat fee. A percentage. A tiered fee. A buyer representation agreement structure. A professional agent has the answer ready in writing and is happy to send it. A non-professional agent gets cagey, defensive, or hops to a phone call to "explain in person." Those are different agents.

The DIY Real Estate Question: Can You Do It On Your Own?

There is always a push to pull the agent out of the transaction entirely. You can do it on your own. The same way that if you get sued, you can play your own attorney and represent yourself in court. It is legal. It is allowed. In most situations the court will warn you that it is not the best idea and politely recommend you go get someone with the diploma and the credentials — but you can choose to ignore that.

Real estate is the same. The DIY percentage is small. It exists. Whether it is a smart choice depends on two things: where you live, and how much risk you are willing to absorb personally.

State Matters — A Lot

Some states have very little paperwork. Some states have a god-awful amount. California is at the heavy end. Residential real estate here is one of the most document-heavy industries in the state. We have:

  • Roughly 42 standard residential disclosures on the seller side, plus or minus a couple depending on the property type.
  • A separate stack of buyer-side disclosures the buyer is required to receive and acknowledge.
  • Agency disclosures, lead-based paint disclosures (for pre-1978 homes), natural hazard disclosure reports, locality-specific addenda, HOA document packages where applicable, Mello-Roos and special assessment disclosures, environmental hazard disclosures, and a host of others.
  • Standard California Residential Purchase Agreement and Joint Escrow Instructions, plus addenda, counteroffers, contingency removals, and notices to perform — each with strict timelines and contractual consequences for missing them.

It is not impossible. It is not even necessarily a bad idea for the right person in the right transaction. But it is real work. If you DIY a California sale, you are doing the disclosure preparation, the marketing, the showings, the offer review, the negotiation, the contingency-period management, the inspection coordination, the request-for-repairs negotiation, and the closing coordination yourself. You can do it. The question is whether the value you save by skipping the agent fee exceeds the value of the time and the risk you are absorbing.

What I Recommend for DIY-Curious Sellers and Buyers

If you are seriously considering DIY, do these three things before you commit:

  1. Read the California Association of Realtors residential purchase agreement end to end, plus the major disclosure forms. Not skim. Read. If your eyes glaze over by page eight, that is data.
  2. Watch real walkthroughs of the process. I have video content covering buyer DIY and FSBO seller DIY scenarios — the actual mechanical steps, in order, with the gotchas marked. There are honest creators in every market who do the same.
  3. Get a transactional real estate attorney lined up before you list or write. You do not need the attorney to run the whole show. You need them on retainer or on standby in case something complicated lands on your desk on a Friday afternoon.

If after all three of those steps you still want to DIY — go. You are an informed adult. If at any of those steps you say, "Actually I want someone who has done this 800 times to handle it," that is also a perfectly legitimate conclusion. That is what an agent worth their commission is for.

The Email Rule That Protects You in Every Transaction

This is the single most underused protective tool in real estate and I want every reader to walk away with it. The rule is simple.

After every meaningful verbal conversation with your lender, your agent, your escrow officer, your title rep, or anyone else involved in a transaction, send an email summarizing what was said and asking them to confirm.

That is it. That is the whole rule. Maybe your lender is your friend. Maybe your lender is your uncle. Maybe your lender is your brother or your sister. Maybe your agent is your cousin. None of that matters. After the conversation, send the email.

Why? Because verbal conversations are not enforceable in any practical way, and human memory is unreliable on purpose. If your lender tells you on the phone that it is fine to pay off the JCPenney card before close, and you do it, and your FICO drops, and the loan does not fund — what do you have? You have a memory. You have nothing to point to. You go back to the lender and you say, "Jack, you told me it was fine." Jack says, "What are you going to do?" And it is over.

Now run the same scenario with an email. After the call, you send: "Jack, just confirming our conversation. You said I can pay off the JCPenney card before close without affecting my approval. Please reply to confirm." Jack replies. You have a record. If the FICO drops anyway, you have a paper trail. If you ever need legal recourse, you have an exhibit. If you do not need any of that, the email cost you sixty seconds and the lender appreciated the professionalism.

The Naked Mole Rat Rule

Here is the most important part of the email rule. Use it for things that seem completely innocent. Use it for things that do not even seem to matter. Use it for things you barely understand.

Let's say you and I have a conversation and at the end of it you are still not sure you heard me right. You send me an email: "Connor, we were talking earlier and I just want to make sure I heard you correctly. You said a naked mole rat could ride a unicorn over a beautiful rainbow through a valley of cotton candy. Did I get that right?"

I come back and say, "Almost. Naked mole rat, yes. Unicorn, yes. Rainbow, yes. But it is a mountain top full of cotton candy, not a valley. The valley reference was something else." Now you have it documented, in writing, with the correction. If the cotton-candy mountain ever becomes a legal issue, you have the exhibit. If it never becomes one, you spent a minute and built a reflex that will save you when the actual stakes are high.

I am not joking when I tell you this is the best protective method you can adopt in a real estate transaction. Do the follow-up. Do the investigation. Make sure you understood what was being conveyed completely. Build the paper trail on the small stuff so it is automatic when the big stuff happens.

Working with an agent who actually puts everything in writing should be the floor, not the ceiling. If you are not getting that today, let's have a conversation about what the listing experience should feel like.

The Buyer-Side Investigation Almost No One Does

I do not represent buyers. I am a sellers-only agent. You can read why at sellersonlyagent.com. But I am going to give you the single most important piece of buyer advice I know, because almost no buyer does it and almost every buyer wishes they had.

Once you are in escrow on a property — once the offer is accepted, the counteroffers are done, you have lined up the inspection, you have read the disclosures, you have done all the formal due diligence the contract requires — there is one more thing on the to-do list. It is not on any document. It is not in any disclosure. It is not in the contract.

Go door-knock the neighbors.

Which Neighbors to Knock On

  • The neighbor on each side of you.
  • The neighbor directly behind you if you share a fence or a property line.
  • If the home is on a corner lot or has unusual adjacency, anyone whose property touches yours.
  • Optionally, the three or four neighbors directly across the street — not because they share a property line, but because you are going to look at them every time you back out of the driveway.

Take Your Agent With You

Bring your buyer's agent. Make them earn their commission for that hour. If they will not go — if they tell you that is awkward or unprofessional or not done — you might be working with the wrong agent. The introduction is part of the job. Your agent gets to meet potential future clients. The neighbor might be thinking about selling. Renters might be moving out. Your agent might walk away with a listing lead, and you get a buyer side of the door-knock visit that does not feel like a stalker situation.

What You Are Listening For

  • Dogs. Did a dog try to come through the door when you knocked? That dog is going to be in the backyard at some point. If he is a barker, you should know now, not on the third night of your new mortgage.
  • Renters or owners? How long have they been there? Just moved in? Moving out? Long-term tenants? The mix of owners and renters in a tract tells you about the long-term character of the street.
  • Kids in a band. I am only half joking. Is there a high schooler with a drum kit next door? Is there a garage practice space? Are the next-door teenagers running a dirt-bike track in the backyard?
  • The party house. You knock and the garage opens and you see the keg setup, the pool table, the fifteen friends, and the marijuana smell rolling out. Maybe that is fine for your lifestyle; maybe it is not. Either way, you should know.
  • The fence situation. Is there a fence between the properties? Is it leaning? Cracked? In poor shape? Who owns which side? This is information you cannot get from the disclosure package and is a conversation you absolutely want to have before you are the legal neighbor.
  • Tree roots. Big trees on the neighboring lot, root systems that might be heading toward your foundation or your plumbing. The neighbor will tell you stories the disclosure package never will.
  • Personal history. I will be blunt: I have had a buyer knock on a door and discover the neighbor was an ex with an active restraining order against them. That is an extreme case. It is also a real case. The door-knock surfaces this stuff while you can still get out of the contract.

The Talker Neighbor Is the Best Neighbor

One of the neighbors you knock on is going to yuck it up. They are going to talk and talk and talk and you are going to have to chew your arm off to escape. That is the neighbor you want. They know everything. They are going to give you the gossip on the street, the personalities, the history, the things that have happened and the things that are about to. Not from a biblical-sinful-gossip standpoint — from an educational standpoint. You are about to live there. Information is what you need.

The Fence Conversation

If you see a shared fence in bad shape, the door-knock is your chance to have a soft conversation about it. "I am buying the place next door. The fence looks like it has seen better days. Down the road, if it needs replacing, do you think you would be open to splitting the cost?"

You probably will not get that in writing in the doorway. You probably will not get a firm commitment of any kind. You might get an awkward "maybe." That is fine. The information you wanted was whether the neighbor is reasonable or unreasonable about shared property issues, and you just got it. If the answer was a hostile no, you now have a data point on whether you want to be neighbors with this person for the next decade.

Why Almost No Agent Recommends This

I will tell you the truth. A lot of agents do not recommend the door-knock because they are worried the buyer is going to change their mind and the deal is going to fall apart. I would rather you change your mind now than call me six months from now and tell me you wish you had. Once you close, I cannot do anything with that information. While we are in escrow, I can help you get out under the contract's contingency periods.

The Recap: Eight Things to Take Away

  1. The 2024 commission lawsuit did not invent the negotiability of commissions. It forced the industry to be transparent about it. That is a good thing for consumers, full stop.
  2. Commission is negotiable on both sides of every transaction. Your agent should be able to walk you through theirs in writing in the first meeting.
  3. Flash agents sell status. Substance agents sell competence. Look at what they post. Look at what they put in writing. Hire the second one.
  4. DIY real estate is legal everywhere. In California it is paperwork-heavy enough that you should know what you are getting into before you commit.
  5. If you are going to DIY, get a transactional real estate attorney on retainer or on standby before you list or write.
  6. Every meaningful verbal conversation with your lender, your agent, or anyone else gets a follow-up email summarizing what was said. Build the habit on small stuff so it is automatic on big stuff.
  7. Before close, door-knock the neighbors on each side and behind. Take your agent with you. Listen for dogs, fences, personalities, and any history you cannot get from the disclosure package.
  8. Protection is the job. Anyone working on your deal who is not protecting you is in the wrong job.

Frequently Asked Questions

What did the 2024 real estate commission lawsuit actually change?

The 2024 settlement made it explicit that real estate commissions are negotiable on both sides of a transaction and that buyers and sellers must be given clear written disclosure of how each side's agent is being paid. Buyer-broker compensation is no longer assumed to come from the listing-side commission split, written buyer representation agreements are now standard before showings in most states, and sellers and buyers have more direct visibility into and control over what they are paying for representation.

Are real estate commissions actually negotiable?

Yes. Commissions have always been legally negotiable. The 2024 lawsuit established that some agents were not disclosing that fact clearly to clients. A professional agent should walk you through the fee, what it covers, what it does not cover, and what alternative fee structures (flat fee, hourly, hybrid) are available. If your agent will not have that conversation in writing, that is a signal about how the rest of the transaction will be handled.

Can I sell my house in California without a real estate agent?

Yes, you can list and sell For Sale By Owner in California. The state requires roughly 42 standard residential disclosures on the seller side alone, plus buyer-side disclosures, agency disclosures, lead-based paint, natural hazard reports, and locality-specific addenda. California is one of the most document-heavy real estate states in the country. FSBO is legal and possible; it is also a significantly more involved DIY project here than in many other states.

What is the most important thing buyers should do before closing escrow on a new home?

Door-knock the neighbors who share a property line with the home you are buying. Meet the people on both sides and behind if you share a fence. You will learn about shared fences in disrepair, dogs that bark, parking situations, and personalities you cannot get from any disclosure document. Bring your agent. Make introductions. This is one of the most underused buyer-side protections in real estate.

What email rule should I follow during a real estate transaction?

After any meaningful verbal conversation with your lender, your agent, or anyone else involved in the transaction, follow up with an email that summarizes what was said and asks them to confirm. That email creates a written record. If something goes wrong later, you have documentation. If it goes right, the email cost you 60 seconds and the recipient appreciates the professionalism.

How can I tell if my real estate agent is worth their commission?

Look at what they post and how they communicate. An agent whose social feeds are dominated by vacations, expensive cars, and lifestyle flexes is selling status. An agent whose feeds are dominated by buyer and seller education, market data, and the protective how-to that helps you avoid mistakes is selling competence. Ask for their marketing plan in writing. Ask how they document their conversations. Ask what alternative fee structures they offer. A worthwhile agent has clear, written answers to all three.

Why did Connor MacIvor stop representing buyers?

After 14 years as a dual-direction agent, Connor went sellers-only in 2019. The reason is conflict avoidance: dual agency, where one agent represents both the seller and the buyer on the same property, dilutes representation on both sides. Going sellers-only means one direction of loyalty and harder-fought negotiations for the seller. The buyer always has their own representation.

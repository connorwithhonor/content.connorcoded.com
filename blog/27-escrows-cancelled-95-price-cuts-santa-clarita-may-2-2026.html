Why 95 Price Reductions Matters More Than the Number Itself

Ninety five price reductions in a single 7 day window in Santa Clarita is not a panic number. It is a correction number. The story behind every one of those 95 cuts is the same story, told 95 ways: the original list price did not match the buyer pool reading the listing.

You will see this almost every market cycle. A property hits the market. Days on market start to climb. The seller starts checking with people in their world: family, friends, the cousin who flipped a house in 2014, the neighbor who sold three years ago. Somebody, somewhere says, "Maybe your agent priced it too low. You should raise the price." And then it happens. The list price goes up.

Once a property shows a price increase on the multiple listing service, I do not really care what market you are in. That is usually detrimental to the seller. The market is reading the price history, and the message a price increase sends is that the seller is making decisions based on hope rather than data. You have to explore that with your agent. Your agent might have a different spin. But I have watched this hurt sellers many times.

A price reduction is the market correcting a list price. A price increase is a list price correcting against the market. The market wins every time.

The 27 Escrow Cancellations: What That Number Is Telling Us

Twenty seven properties cancelled escrow this week and went back on market. That is a meaningful number. It tells you that buyers are doing what buyers should do in a market that is not on fire: inspecting carefully, watching their appraisals, walking when something does not pencil, and refusing to overpay because they are not under the same time pressure they were in 2021 and 2022.

If you are listing a home right now, you need to plan for this. The deal is not done at acceptance. The deal is done at recording. The window between those two events has more failure points than at any time in the last four years. Pricing right at list, prepping the home for inspection rather than hoping the buyer will not look hard, and being prepared to negotiate on credits at the request for repairs are the three behaviors that separate the 38 properties that closed escrow this week from the 27 that did not.

The Two Pending Statuses Most Sellers Have Never Heard Of

You are going to see two different statuses on properties this week, and I want you to understand them because they matter when you are buying or selling. There are two types of "in escrow" statuses an agent can select in the multiple listing service.

Active Under Contract

The first is "active under contract." On many third party syndication sites, these properties might still look like they are showing as available. The seller is still encouraging showings. If you find one and you love it, you can still write an offer. Your agent will submit it as a back up. Forty six properties are sitting in this status right now in Santa Clarita.

Buyers usually skip these because the home looks "in escrow" on Zillow or Redfin, but a smart buyer with a sharp agent will keep watching. If the first deal blows up, and we just told you 27 of those happened this week, the back up is in line.

Pending

The other status is "pending." This is the one where the seller has accepted the offer and the property is no longer encouraging showings. Some syndication sites will still advertise these and pull buyers in for what is essentially a closed door. The "trick" you sometimes see, and I am not accusing every agent of this, is that pending listings continue to attract leads on portals because they look fully available. A buyer clicks. An out of area agent gets the lead. The buyer does not get the house.

If you are working with a sharp local agent, you will know the difference between active under contract and pending the moment you ask about a property. If you are not, you will spend half your weekend driving past houses that are not actually available.

The 53 Sales and the 14 On Hold

Fifty three properties closed escrow this week. That is a healthy number for early May. Closings are a backward looking indicator, written 30 to 45 days ago. They tell you what was happening in mid March. Today's market is being written in the 38 new pending sales and the 95 reductions.

Fourteen properties were placed on hold. That number tends to be a mix of homes that need a refresh, homes pulled while sellers reconsider, and homes pulled to time the market. None of those decisions are bad. Sometimes the right move for a seller is to pull the listing, paint, replace carpet, and come back in 30 days at the right list price.

The San Fernando Valley to Santa Clarita Migration Story

Now we get to the part that the data alone does not tell you. A lot of the buyers behind those 38 new pendings and 53 closings are not Santa Clarita locals trading laterally. They are San Fernando Valley families making a calculated move north, and the math behind that move is the most underrated story in Los Angeles County housing right now.

The Price Per Square Foot Reality

Santa Clarita Valley is currently trading around $425 per square foot on average across the major communities. That number flexes by neighborhood. Stevenson Ranch sits higher because of the schools and the curb appeal. Castaic and parts of Canyon Country sit lower because of inventory mix and lot configuration. But $425 is the working number for a typical 2,400 to 3,000 square foot single family home in 2026.

Now compare that to the San Fernando Valley:

  • Sherman Oaks: roughly $697 per square foot
  • Encino: low to mid $700s per square foot
  • Studio City: $750 plus per square foot
  • San Fernando proper: $536 per square foot
  • Northridge and Reseda: mid $500s per square foot

For a family looking at a 3,000 square foot home, that is the difference between a $1.275 million purchase in Santa Clarita and a $2.09 million purchase in Sherman Oaks. Same square footage. Same family. Eight hundred and fifteen thousand dollars in mortgage delta.

At a 6.75 percent thirty year fixed rate on the difference alone, that is roughly $5,290 per month. About $63,500 per year. After tax, you are looking at well over $90,000 of pre tax income that does not have to be earned to own the same amount of house.

The School Story Is Doing the Real Work

Price per square foot is the headline. Schools are the trigger. The single most common reason families relocate from Sherman Oaks, Encino, Studio City, and Northridge to Santa Clarita is the school decision tree.

In the San Fernando Valley, the dominant pattern for families who can afford it is private school. Harvard Westlake, Buckley, Campbell Hall, Curtis, and the dozens of high quality independent and parochial schools in the Valley are excellent. They also cost between $30,000 and $80,000 per student per year by 2026. Two kids and you are looking at $60,000 to $160,000 of after tax tuition before you have paid your mortgage.

Now look at Santa Clarita schools.

  • Stevenson Ranch Elementary is a three time National Blue Ribbon School. That is one of the highest federal designations a public school can earn, and Stevenson Ranch has earned it three separate times.
  • Arroyo Seco Junior High was named a 2025 California Blue Ribbon School.
  • On April 30, 2026, two days ago, seven Santa Clarita Valley schools received the 2026 California Distinguished Schools designation, one of the highest state level honors a public school can receive.
  • West Ranch High, Valencia High, Saugus High, and Golden Valley High consistently produce strong UC and Cal State acceptance rates and rank competitively against any public high schools in southern California.

So now run the math. A Sherman Oaks family with two kids in private school is paying around $90,000 per year in tuition while sitting on a $2 million plus mortgage. They sell their home, move to Stevenson Ranch, buy a comparable home for around $1.2 million, and put their kids in some of the best public schools in southern California. They keep the $90,000 of after tax tuition. They also save roughly $5,000 a month in mortgage payment. They are roughly $150,000 a year better off, and the kids are at schools that have been federally and state recognized for academic performance.

That is not a soft argument. That is the math behind the migration.

The Bitter Pill: Commute

Nothing about this is free. The trade you have to swallow if you move from the Valley to Santa Clarita is the commute. The 5 freeway between the SR 14 split and the 405 interchange is one of the most reliably congested corridors in southern California in the morning and the evening.

This is a personal decision. Some families never make the math work because the commute breaks them. Some families make the math work brilliantly because they restructure their work life around it.

Here is what I have seen work for families who made the move and stayed happy:

  • One spouse with a remote or hybrid arrangement, the other commuting two or three days a week.
  • Audiobooks, language apps, and podcasts. People sometimes learn an entire language in the time they spend in southbound traffic.
  • Off peak schedules. Leaving Santa Clarita at 5:30 a.m. or 6:00 a.m. takes the commute from 70 minutes to 35 minutes.
  • Metrolink. The Antelope Valley Line runs from Santa Clarita to Union Station and connects to the Red Line. Time on the train is time you can work.

If you are a family where both spouses have to be in office Monday through Friday in Beverly Hills or Century City by 8:00 a.m., the math is still real but the commute will be the cost. You have to weigh that against $90,000 to $150,000 a year of recovered cash flow.

What This Means If You Are Buying in Santa Clarita Right Now

This is one of the better buying environments Santa Clarita has seen in three years. Ninety five price reductions and 27 cancelled escrows mean that sellers who are still on market right now are increasingly willing to negotiate. Buyer leverage is real, and it is not coming from a crash. It is coming from a normalization.

  • Use a buyer agent who knows the Santa Clarita inventory at the neighborhood level. Stevenson Ranch is not Castaic. Saugus is not Newhall. The price per square foot ranges, school boundaries, HOA structures, and Mello Roos differ meaningfully.
  • Ask your agent to show you the active under contract list, not just the active list. Forty six homes are sitting in that status. Some of those will fall out. The first call after a fall out wins.
  • Order an aggressive inspection. The buyer pool is doing this, and that is part of why escrows are cancelling. Your inspection is leverage at the request for repairs.
  • Get pre approved with a local lender who has closed in the SCV. Not your cousin in Phoenix. Not the rocket app. A lender whose underwriter knows how to read a Mello Roos disclosure and a community facilities district line item.

What This Means If You Are Selling in Santa Clarita Right Now

You can absolutely sell in this market. You cannot afford to mis price. The 95 reductions and the 27 cancellations both trace back, in most cases, to one of two errors at listing: the price was wrong, or the property was not prepared for the inspection.

  • Price at the market, not at the dream. Your agent should walk you through the last 90 days of comparable sales, not the active listings. Active listings are aspirations. Sales are evidence.
  • Pre inspect the home. A pre listing inspection costs $400 to $700 and lets you know what the buyer is going to find before they find it. You can fix it, disclose it, or price it in. All three are better than getting blindsided on day 17.
  • Stage. Even a light stage with the existing furniture rearranged and decluttered moves the needle on photos and showings.
  • Pick an agent who shows you a marketing plan in writing, with the line items, the costs, and the deliverables. If your agent does not put it in writing, the marketing might not happen.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Santa Clarita a good place to move from the San Fernando Valley?

For families prioritizing public school quality, price per square foot, and quality of life, Santa Clarita is one of the best value moves in Los Angeles County. The trade is the commute. If at least one earner has remote, hybrid, or off peak flexibility, the math typically works. If both earners are required to be in mid Wilshire or downtown by 8:00 a.m. five days a week, you have to weigh the daily commute cost against the annual cash flow recovery.

How are Santa Clarita schools rated compared to San Fernando Valley public schools?

Santa Clarita schools, particularly in the Stevenson Ranch, Valencia, and Saugus areas, have earned multiple National Blue Ribbon and California Blue Ribbon designations, including a 2025 California Blue Ribbon for Arroyo Seco Junior High and seven 2026 California Distinguished Schools designations announced on April 30, 2026. The local high schools post strong UC and Cal State acceptance rates. Many SFV families who would otherwise pay private school tuition find the SCV public school option meets or exceeds their academic standards.

What is the average price per square foot in Santa Clarita right now?

Roughly $425 per square foot across the SCV in early May 2026, with neighborhood variation. Stevenson Ranch sits higher, parts of Castaic and Canyon Country sit lower. By comparison, Sherman Oaks is around $697 per square foot and Studio City is north of $750 per square foot.

What does it mean when a home is "active under contract" versus "pending"?

Active under contract means the seller has accepted an offer but is still encouraging showings and back up offers. Pending means the seller is no longer accepting showings. There are 46 active under contract homes in Santa Clarita this week. Smart buyers monitor that list because some of them will return to active.

Why did 27 escrows cancel this week in Santa Clarita?

Cancellations cluster around three causes: appraisal coming in below contract price, inspection findings the seller will not credit, and buyer financing changes. In a normalizing market, buyers feel less pressure to overlook issues, and that pushes the cancellation rate up. The fix on the seller side is to price right and pre inspect.

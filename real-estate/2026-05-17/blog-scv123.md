## Inventory Is Climbing, But the Sale Pace Isn't — Your May 17, 2026 Real Estate Update

Welcome to the mid-May 2026 read. Today's headline is a two-parter: **active inventory is up**, but the **speed of absorption is essentially flat** versus the close of 2025.

In San Francisco alone we have roughly **200 more active units for sale** than we did just a couple of months ago. That's a real shift on the supply side. What hasn't shifted is how quickly those listings are being absorbed. Buyers are out there. Closings are happening. But the pace is slower than what most of us had penciled in for spring.

### Why mortgage rates aren't cooperating

The short answer: the **10-year Treasury bond**.

When investors lose confidence in the 10-year — and right now plenty have, thanks to ongoing wars, oil pressure, and fuel-market jitters — they aren't buying that bond hand over fist. That lack of demand pushes yields up, and mortgage rates ride right along with it. The current consolation prize? Higher rates.

When the world settles down (and it will, eventually), bond buying typically resumes, yields ease, and mortgage rates start drifting back down.

There's a second wildcard worth watching: a **new Fed chair has been confirmed**. Once they're seated, you can expect some recalibration on the policy side. Whether that's hawkish, dovish, or somewhere in between, it'll move the market. Keep an eye on it.

### Buyers: a word on contingencies

If you're writing offers in this market, talk to your agent about your **investigation contingency**.

In a roaring-hot market, agents will sometimes coach buyers to **drop every contingency** — appraisal, loan, investigation — to win the bid. The moment escrow opens, that deposit is on the hook. Immediately. No safety net. You haven't seen the disclosures. You haven't brought an inspector through. You have no idea what the property entails beyond your first walk-through.

Could there be something on the disclosures you're not OK with? A quadruple-triple-murder-suicide? (I'm kidding. Mostly.) The point stands: **disclosures and inspections exist for a reason**.

Now, that doesn't mean there's no way to move fast. Back when Santa Clarita was at its peak, plenty of buyers waived contingencies — and a meaningful chunk of them mitigated it by **bringing a home inspector to the showing itself**. They saw what they were getting before they wrote. That's a legitimate middle path. There's more than one way to skin this.

But right now, this is **not that market**. So if your agent is pushing zero-contingency offers in May 2026, ask why.

### Sellers: get the valuation right

If you're listing in the back half of May, your single most important pre-launch decision is **pricing the property correctly**. Get the valuation right.

Here's the trap most sellers fall into: they punch their address into Zillow, Redfin, or Realtor.com to "check the number." Sounds harmless. It isn't.

The moment you submit your address — **even without your name or email** — your property is flagged. AI systems across the syndication ecosystem (Zillow, Redfin, Realtor.com, and the rest) start monitoring it. Your property becomes a **target**. Your information gets tabulated and routed into lead-distribution systems. Then you'll watch your social feeds light up with real estate ads aimed straight at you.

Some of those platforms are technically "Realtor" sites. In my experience, **loyalty follows the pocketbook** more than the badge. Treat the online valuation tools as marketing funnels — not as your appraisal.

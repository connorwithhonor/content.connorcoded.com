Call the Sheriff Before You Buy: SCV Inventory Hits 722 + The Buyer Recon Most Agents Won't Teach You

May 16, 2026 · By Connor Mac · Santa Clarita Valley Market Update

Good day, everybody. Connor Mac here with your Santa Clarita Valley townhome inventory update for May 16.

We've officially crossed 722 total active townhomes across every SCV city — Valencia, Saugus, Canyon Country, Newhall, Stevenson Ranch, and Castaic. That's a meaningful jump from where we sat just a couple of months back, when we were hovering around 520. Classic spring-and-summer movement: families jockeying for position before the school year, sellers timing the bounce, and buyers finally getting a little breathing room on choices.

But this post isn't really about the inventory number. It's about what you should actually be doing as a buyer before you write an offer — the recon work most agents will never put into a glossy "why this neighborhood is amazing" video.

You'll see plenty of agents pushing videos hand over fist about how great the schools are, how safe the community is, how wonderful life is in Valencia. That's fine. But you need to do your own recon.

Call the Sheriff's Department Before You Buy

This is the former cop in me talking — and I was actually one, so take it for what it's worth.

If you've narrowed your search down to a community — Bridgeport, North Park, Northbridge, a Valencia village, Canyon Country, Saugus, Castaic — pick up the phone and call the local SCV Sheriff's station before you write anything.

  • Don't call Friday night at 8 p.m. Try Monday midday.
  • Ask for the watch deputy or lead deputy on shift.
  • Say: "I'm thinking about buying a home in [neighborhood]. Can you tell me anything about it from a crime perspective? Anything going on I should know about?"

They will tell you things no listing agent ever will.

The "David in Burbank" Story

In 1993, I bought a house in Burbank, off Magnolia. Down the street lived a guy named David.

David liked to wear welding gloves to 7-Eleven so he could carry a giant Diet Dr. Pepper without freezing his hands. David also liked to stand outside neighbors' windows at 2 or 3 in the morning and scream when he thought you were bothering him.

If I had called Burbank PD before closing, they would have warned me: "David lives down the street. Mostly harmless. But every once in a while he'll yell outside your window at 3 a.m. for a couple of hours."

Would that have changed my decision? Maybe. But at least I would have known.

There's a David on more streets than you'd think. Make the call.

Knock on the Neighbors' Doors

Sellers and listing agents won't tell you about:

  • The garage band practicing drums at 11 p.m. two doors down
  • The dog that barks at 7 a.m. every single morning
  • The boyfriend with a restraining order
  • Tree roots from next door cracking the shared block wall
  • A leaning fence nobody wants to pay to fix
  • The upslope neighbor who can see straight into your backyard pool

A 10-minute walk and a few polite door knocks will tell you more than a 45-minute open house ever will.

Pro tip: Have the awkward conversation BEFORE you close

If you see massive tree roots pushing through your future fence, knock on the neighbor's door:

"Hey, I'm thinking about buying next door. I noticed the fence is leaning where the roots are coming through. Has the current owner ever talked about that with you?"

You can only believe people so far — but at least you've made the contact. If they slam the door in your face, you've still learned something valuable about your future neighbor.

Vet Your Home Inspector — Don't Just Take the Default

Don't just hire the inspector your agent always uses. Ask:

  • How long have you been doing this?
  • Do you have a track record? References?
  • What happens if you miss something?
  • Have you ever had a deal go sideways because of your report?

Some inspectors get the "go-to" spot because they write reports nicely and don't rock the boat. Great for the agent's commission. Terrible for you when the roof leaks three months after closing.

Read the report line by line

When you get the report back, read every line. Modern reports are 100+ pages with 150+ photos and things get sloppy. If page 23 says "petrified rat droppings found in the far corner of the attic" but the accompanying photo is a picture of the sun — call the inspector. Ask what they meant. Then get the answer in writing.

Get Everything in Writing

Your buyer's agent. Your lender. Your inspector. Your insurance pro. Follow up every important conversation with:

"Hey, just confirming what we discussed on the phone — you said XYZ. Is that correct?"

The trust thing matters. Paper trails matter more. If it's a relative, make them put it in an email twice.

Right now we have 22 Coming Soon townhome listings in the SCV.

These are properties that, per MLS policy, are not syndicated to Zillow, Realtor.com, or Redfin. The only way to see them is if your agent emails them to you directly.

  • Low end: ~$679,000
  • High end: ~$1.9 million (Castaic)

If you have a buyer's agent, call them today: "Make sure I'm getting Coming Soon listings as they hit, filtered by my criteria."

Stay disciplined — don't chase listings $200K above your comfort zone unless you genuinely have the discipline to walk away. Most people don't.

Check Insurance in Week ONE — Not Week Three

This is where California buyers get burned the most in 2026. Carriers have pulled out of wildfire zones. The California FAIR Plan is now a real conversation. Some neighborhoods that look perfectly insurable from the curb turn out to be a nightmare to cover.

Handle insurance the moment you settle on an area — not in week three of escrow.

My referral (no kickback, he's just good): Scott Wilk at TWIG Insurance.

Ask:

  • Is this address insurable by a standard carrier, or are we looking at FAIR Plan?
  • What's the deductible, and what does it cover on a total loss?
  • Can I bundle with my auto policy (AAA, etc.)?
  • If I lose the whole house, does the policy rebuild what I have today — or am I left moving a single-wide onto a 3,000 sq ft lot?

The Bottom Line

  • Call the Sheriff's Department.
  • Knock on the neighbors' doors.
  • Vet your inspector.
  • Get everything in writing.
  • Get on the Coming Soon list.
  • Check insurance in week one.

Watch out for yourselves. Be good to each other.
